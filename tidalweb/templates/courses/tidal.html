Description

Emerging interactive technologies are rapidly transforming the ways in which we work, play, communicate, and learn. Research on tangible interaction attempts to blur the line between digital technologies and the broader physical, social, and cultural worlds within which computer use is situated. This course will explore the use of tangible interaction to create innovative learning experiences. It will review both theoretical and technological foundations of the field. Topics include distributed cognition, embodied interaction, cultural forms, and design frameworks.

Coursework will consist of weekly readings, discussions, and labs. Students will also work in small teams to complete a final paper and project on the topic of virtuosity and creativity. Teams will revise their work several times over the course of the term and receive feedback from their peers. This course is intended for students interested in publishing in learning sciences, human-computer interaction, or related fields.

Attendance and Participation

You are expected to complete the assigned readings and participate in discussions. Attendance at all classes is required, and unexcused absences will directly affect your class participation grades.

Leading Class Discussion

Every student will be responsible for leading class discussion once during the term around the week's readings. For your presentation you should provide a brief biographical sketch of the main authors, present an overview of the papers, and pose questions or provocative statements to stimulate discussion. You will also share an example tangible interface taken from the ACM Digital Library.

Lab Work

Every week you will participate in two-hour lab sessions during class time. The goal of the labs is to build your expertise with various tangible prototyping technologies including 3D printing, embedded computing, computer vision, depth cameras, and wireless technology. Some extra work outside of class time might be required to complete the labs.

Final Project

Over the course of the term you will work on a final paper and demo video on the topic of virtuosity and creativity. Students will work in interdisciplinary teams of three to four members. Final papers should be 6 pages long and formatted in the ACM HCI extended abstracts format. The final projects must be working tangible interface prototypes consisting of some blend of hardware and software. These projects need not be fully featured, but the core functionality should be there. No textbooks are required for this course, but students will be required to purchase some basic prototyping equipment that will be contributed to their group.

Assignment 1: Teams and Topics
Tangible Interaction Design and Learning (Spring 2015)

Overview

The Northwestern quarter system doesn't give us much spare time, so we're going to dive right in to the first team assignment. Your first job will be to find three teammates to work with this quarter. Your team should include at least one person with experience programming / hacking / making and at least one person with experience in the social sciences (learning sciences, communication, sociology, psychology, and so on). Please let me know if you have any trouble finding people to work with.

Your next job will be to come up with a list of three or four project ideas at the intersection of learning and tangible interaction. I define both of these terms very broadly, and we'll have a chance to discuss these in class in more depth.

Assigned:
April 1

Due:
April 8

Worth:
0% of your grade

What to Hand In

Submit a short document to Canvas. The document should contain a list of team members with a short description of each person's relevant expertise including academic major. Include relevant skills like graphical design, visual arts, product design, engineering, etc. Next, describe each of your design ideas with one or two paragraphs each.

Lab 1: 3D Printing
Tangible Interaction Design and Learning (Spring 2015)

Overview

In this lab you will create a 3D printed object. The goal is for all teams to have some expertise in creating printed objects as a valuable tool for your final projects.

Assigned:
April 1

Due:
April 8

To complete the lab do the following:

  1. Create a tinkercad account (tinkercad).
  2. Browse the gallery to get inspired and find starting points for your project.
  3. Create your own model using the cad software.
    • Important: keep your models small (small trinket sized). The whole class is sharing one printer, so it's not feasible to create large or multi-part objects for this lab.
    • You can start with an existing model, but make sure you make significant modifications. The point is for you to develop expertise with this tool.

What to Hand In

Download your model as an STL file and submit it to Canvas. We'll try to print some of your objects before next week.
